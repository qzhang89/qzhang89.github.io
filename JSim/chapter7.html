Schrödinger equation in spherical coordinates

In spherical coordinates, the time-independent Schrödinger equation reads (assume \( V \) depends only on \( r \))

\[ -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m} \left[ \frac{1}{r^2} \frac{\partial}{\partial r} \left( r^2 \frac{\partial \psi}{\partial r} \right) + \frac{1}{r^2 \sin{\theta}} \frac{\partial}{\partial \theta} \left( \sin{\theta} \frac{\partial \psi}{\partial \theta} \right) + \frac{1}{r^2 \sin^2{\theta}} \left( \frac{\partial^2 \psi}{\partial \phi^2} \right) \right] + V(r) \psi = E \psi \]

By separation of variables

\[ \psi(r, \theta, \phi) = R(r) Y(\theta, \phi) \]

We obtain our radial equation for \( R(r) \) and angular equation for \( Y(\theta, \phi) \)

\[ \begin{aligned} \text{Radial equation: } & \frac{1}{R} \frac{d}{d r} \left( r^2 \frac{d R}{d r} \right) - \frac{2mr^2}{\hbar^2} [V(r) - E] = l(l+1) \\ \text{Angular equation: } & - \frac{1}{Y} \left[ \frac{1}{\sin{\theta}} \frac{\partial}{\partial \theta} \left( \sin{\theta} \frac{\partial Y}{\partial \theta} \right) + \frac{1}{\sin^2{\theta}} \frac{\partial^2 Y}{\partial \phi^2} \right] = -l(l+1) \end{aligned} \]

Radial equation

The solution for the angular eqution is shown in Chapter 4 - Spherical Harmonics. Here, we solve the radial equation. This equation simplifies if we change variables:

\[ u(r) \equiv rR(r) \]

The radial equation now becomes:

\[ \text{Radial equation: } - \frac{\hbar^2}{2m} \frac{d^2u}{dx^2} + \left[ V(r) + \frac{\hbar^2}{2m} \frac{l(l+1)}{r^2} \right] u = E u \]

This equation is almost identical to the one-dimensional Schrödinger equation, except we now have an extra term \( (\hbar^2/2m)[l(l+1)/r^2] \) in addition to the potential \( V(r) \).

Meanwhile, note that these two normalization conditions are equivalent:

\[ \int_0^{\infty} |R|^2 r^2 dr = 1 \quad \text{and} \quad \int_0^{\infty} |u|^2 dr = 1 \]

For a hydrogen atom, we have a simple Coulomb potential:

\[ V(r) = - \frac{e^2}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{1}{r} \]
